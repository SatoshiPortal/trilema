So how about cracking it for ourselves, with our own two hands ? It's almost as if we'd be learning a language, after all. How'd we go about it ?

Reasonably, the first thing we do is we replace the "mysterious symbols, some of them resembling Cyrillic, Greek and ancient Egyptian" with plain old latin letters, using a new one each time we encounter a new symbol. As such :

a bcde feg bcijg jke lemmng a bjebajg
gc oajjeppc gq qcmaqe erckdc icmgeja mcde
oajjeppa labc e rckdala bc g bcijkg jke
lemmng a bjebajg ie qckc oajjeppa la bc mcb
rckkg majece e madgmge fg rckpa gc
oajjeppc e rckdc mcb rekkg majebe e
madgmge fg rckpa ie qckc oajjeppa la bc

We've still got stuvwxyz unused, and moreover they're really doing us a lot of favours. Consider the oajjeppa/oajjeppc flection, or lemmng invariance plus all the fixed single and two letters, bc, a, e, ie, la, gc etc. Not to mention the fragments repeated verbatim, madgmge fg rckpa etc. Even if if this weren't a simple letter cypher but used instead syllables (like the actual hieroglyphic notation) we'd suss out the latin substrate of the language within this first paragraph.

This already gives us a generally good idea what's what, but there's one valuable pattern to consider first : the double consonant beauty. Specifically, our text contains double m, j, p and k, but also a very valuable word with two doubles, oajjeppc. At this juncture it'd seem reasonable that we should construct a list of all Italian words that match this otherwise very stringent pattern. How many do you think they are ? Like a few, a dozen tops, right ? Maybe a hundred, if you happen to know that Italian doubles any consonant it gets its hands on. Well... let me spare you the guessing : 633 exactly.v
